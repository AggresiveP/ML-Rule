An interactive, educational dashboard and sandbox designed to demystify the "Black Box" of Artificial Intelligence. RULEX translates complex neural network boundaries into clear, human-readable rules, enabling auditability, trust, and legal compliance.

The Challenge // Distrust & Regulations

When algorithms make high-stakes decisions—like rejecting credit applications, screening resumes, or assessing patient health—their reasoning is hidden behind millions of unreadable mathematical calculations (the "Black Box"). Distrust in these systems has led to strict legal mandates: GDPR Article 22 guarantees individuals the "Right to an Explanation", and the EU AI Act requires mandatory audit transparency.

The Solution // Rule Extraction Sandbox

RULEX bridges the gap by translating dense neural networks into plain logical rulesets (e.g. "IF Age > 35 AND BMI > 30 THEN Class Diabetic"). You can interactively train models, check fidelity metrics, identify decision discrepancies, and immediately compile the rules into production database code.

Stage 1: The Detective in the Black Box

The Challenge: Imagine a bank uses an advanced Artificial Intelligence (AI) model to approve small-business loans. Elena, a local bakery owner with an excellent credit score, is suddenly rejected by the algorithm. When she asks why, the engineering team opens the model, but they are met with a "black box" containing millions of abstract numbers (weights) and mathematical transformations. Nobody can explain why the decision was made. This lack of explanation violates legal guidelines like GDPR Article 22, which guarantees individuals the right to understand automated decisions.

The Solution: This website acts as a digital detective. Rule Extraction is the technique of translating the massive, unreadable calculations inside the AI into simple, logical rules that any human can understand: IF income < 20,000 AND debt > 50,000 THEN reject loan. Our goal is to achieve Explainable AI (XAI) to build trust, safety, and compliance.

Mathematical Formulation: In multi-layer neural network architectures (Multi-Layer Perceptrons), input features \(x \in \mathbb{R}^d\) are mapped to predictions \(\hat{y}\) through weight matrices \(W_l\), bias vectors \(b_l\), and non-linear activation functions \(\sigma\): \[\hat{y} = f(x) = \sigma\left(W_2 \cdot \tanh(W_1 \cdot x + b_1) + b_2\right)\] While this high-dimensional mapping yields exceptional predictive accuracy, the millions of parameters make manual verification mathematically impossible. The primary goal of rule extraction is to approximate this complex decision boundary \(f(x)\) with an interpretable surrogate function \(g(x)\) composed of discrete logical conjuncts, enabling compliance audits under GDPR Article 22.

There are three academic standard methodologies used for rule extraction:

  • Pedagogical (Black-Box): Treats the neural network as a closed system, modeling its input-output behavior.
  • Decompositional (White-Box): Directly extracts rules by analyzing weights and activation thresholds of individual neurons.
  • Eclectic / Hybrid: Combines elements of both, leveraging inner structure and global outputs.

Stage 2: Global Surrogates (Pedagogical Extraction)

The Concept: A "Global Surrogate" maps the overall logic of the AI. Think of it like "interviewing an expert." Since the AI is a black box, we cannot look inside its brain. Instead, we ask it hundreds of questions (feed it test cases), watch its answers ("Approved" or "Denied"), and write down a simplified rulebook (a Decision Tree) that mimics its behavior.

The Trade-Off (Fidelity vs. Complexity): We measure how closely our rulebook matches the AI using a metric called Fidelity.

  • If we write a short rulebook (e.g., 2 rules), it is easy for a human to read, but the Fidelity will be low (the rules won't match the AI's actual decisions very well).
  • If we write a huge rulebook (e.g., 50 rules), it will match the AI perfectly (high fidelity), but it will be impossible for a human to read. The slider on our site lets you find the perfect balance!

Methodology: A Pedagogical global surrogate model \(g(x)\) (such as a Decision Tree) is trained directly on the predictions \(\hat{y} = f(x)\) generated by the black-box neural network, rather than the true labels \(y\). The tree is built using greedy recursive partitioning to maximize **Information Gain** (using Shannon Entropy \(H(T)\)) at each split point: \[H(T) = -\sum_{i=1}^C p_i \log_2 p_i\] \[IG(T, a) = H(T) - \sum_{v \in \text{values}(a)} \frac{|T_v|}{|T|} H(T_v)\] The **Surrogate Fidelity** calculates the degree to which \(g(x)\) approximates the neural network's decision boundary: \[\text{Fidelity}(g; f) = \frac{1}{|D|} \sum_{x \in D} \mathbb{I}(g(x) = f(x))\] By controlling the "Max Depth" hyperparameter, you explore the Pareto frontier of explanation complexity vs. fidelity. Regions of conflict are highlighted as *Decision Discrepancies*.

Stage 3: Local Anchors (Decompositional & Hybrid Focus)

The Concept: Sometimes explaining the whole model is too difficult. Instead, we only want to explain one single case (like Elena's loan rejection). Local Anchors solve this by zooming in on a single data point. The system makes hundreds of minor changes to Elena's profile (e.g., slightly altering income, debt, and credit score) and tests them against the AI.

It searches for the shortest, most powerful rule (an "Anchor") that guarantees the AI will give the same prediction (rejection) in 95% of those modified profiles. It creates a localized boundary box around her profile, giving a highly precise explanation for that individual decision without needing to explain the entire global network.

Mathematical Definition: An Anchor represents a local rule constraint \(A\) such that if an instance \(x\) satisfies \(A\) (i.e. \(A(x) = 1\)), the black-box model \(f(x)\) yields the same prediction with high probability \(\ge \tau\) (typically \(\tau = 95\%\)) under a localized perturbation distribution \(\mathcal{D}_x(z|A)\): \[\mathbb{P}_{z \sim \mathcal{D}_x(z|A)} [f(x) = f(z)] \ge \tau\] Optimization Strategy: Finding the coarsest rule \(A\) that satisfies this precision constraint is formulated as a Multi-Armed Bandit problem. Using the KL-LUCB algorithm, we sample local perturbations to bound the precision confidence interval, maximizing the **Coverage** (percentage of the global dataset matching the rule): \[\text{Cov}(A) = \mathbb{P}_{z \sim \mathcal{D}} [A(z) = 1]\]

Stage 4: RuleFit Coefficients (Eclectic Method)

The Concept: Instead of building a single decision tree, what if we could combine many rules? RuleFit first extracts hundreds of rules from a large forest of decision trees. However, displaying all of them would be overwhelming. To fix this, the system runs a mathematical filter (called a Lasso filter) that evaluates all rules and raw features together.

It discards redundant or weak rules, leaving behind a compact set of highly influential rules. Each rule is assigned a positive or negative coefficient (weight), showing exactly which rules push the model to approve or reject, capturing non-linear interactions between variables.

Formulation: RuleFit combines linear feature terms \(x_j\) and tree-derived conjunct rules \(r_k(x) \in \{0,1\}\) into a single sparse linear model: \[\hat{y}(x) = \beta_0 + \sum_{j=1}^p \beta_j x_j + \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k r_k(x)\] To extract only the most predictive rules and enforce extreme sparsity, we solve the objective function with a Lasso (\(L_1\) norm) regularization penalty \(\lambda\): \[\min_{\beta, \alpha} \left\{ \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \mathcal{L}(y_i, \hat{y}(i)) + \lambda \left( \sum_{j=1}^p |\beta_j| + \sum_{k=1}^K |\alpha_k| \right) \right\}\] This eclectic approach merges neural net predictions, ensemble-generated path structures, and linear models to display active coefficients.

Stage 5: Governance, Auditing & Deployment

The Goal: Having the rules is only the first step. In production, we must perform a **compliance audit** to verify that the AI is fair, unbiased, and legal. For example, we check if the extracted rules rely on sensitive attributes like gender or age to make decisions (which is illegal under EU AI Act guidelines).

The Compilation: Once audited, we compile the logical rules directly into standard programming code like **Python functions** or **SQL database queries**. This code runs at lightning speed directly inside production databases, replacing the complex neural network with transparent, auditable code that guarantees safety and compliance.

Deployment Pipeline: Approved rules are serialized into PMML (Predictive Model Markup Language) XML schemas or exported as high-performance database case constructs: 

CASE WHEN age < 30 AND debt > 50000 THEN 0 ELSE 1 END
This governance step audits the extracted rules for demographic parity and fairness. By executing compiled SQL queries directly on production databases, it bypasses the runtime latencies, execution environments, and uninterpretable black-box pathways of deep neural networks, meeting both strict engineering SLA requirements and modern AI compliance acts.

Global Surrogate (Fidelity & Information Gain)

The surrogate model \(g(x)\) (a Decision Tree) is trained on the dataset \(D\) using the labels predicted by the black-box model \(f(x)\). The Fidelity represents the classification accuracy of \(g(x)\) on the predictions of \(f(x)\):

\[\text{Fidelity}(g; f) = \frac{1}{|D|} \sum_{x \in D} \mathbb{I}(g(x) = f(x))\]

To split nodes, the tree calculates the **Shannon Entropy** \(H(T)\) and **Information Gain** \(IG(T, a)\) for a split candidate \(a\):

\[H(T) = -\sum_{i=1}^C p_i \log_2 p_i\]
\[IG(T, a) = H(T) - \sum_{v \in \text{values}(a)} \frac{|T_v|}{|T|} H(T_v)\]

Local Anchors (Perturbation & Precision Constraint)

An Anchor is a rule \(A\) such that if an instance \(x\) satisfies \(A\), then the black-box model \(f(x)\) predicts the same label with high probability. The local search optimization must satisfy the precision threshold \(\tau\) (e.g., \(95\%\)):

\[\mathbb{P}_{z \sim \mathcal{D}_x(z|A)} [f(x) = f(z)] \ge \tau\]

Where \(\mathcal{D}_x(z|A)\) is the local perturbation distribution restricted to the rule space \(A\). The coverage represents the percentage of overall data points matching the rule:

\[\text{Cov}(A) = \mathbb{P}_{z \sim \mathcal{D}} [A(z) = 1]\]

RuleFit (Lasso Sparsity Minimization)

RuleFit fits a sparse linear model combining the raw features \(x_j\) and the extracted tree rules \(r_k(x)\):

\[\hat{y}(x) = \beta_0 + \sum_{j=1}^p \beta_j x_j + \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k r_k(x)\]

To select only the most critical rules, we optimize the Lasso objective function with an \(L_1\) regularization penalty \(\lambda\):

\[\min_{\beta, \alpha} \left\{ \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \mathcal{L}(y_i, \hat{y}(i)) + \lambda \left( \sum_{j=1}^p |\beta_j| + \sum_{k=1}^K |\alpha_k| \right) \right\}\]
